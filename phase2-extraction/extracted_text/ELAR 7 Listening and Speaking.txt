Persuasive writing is a type of writing where the author tries to convince the reader to agree with their opinion, believe an idea, or take a specific action. The goal is to influence readers' thoughts or behavior using logical arguments, emotional appeals, facts, and credible sources, including primary documents, professional research, and data. Persuasive or argumentative writing uses rhetorical devices or appeals. These appeals include credibility (ethos), emotional (pathos), and logical (logos) appeals, but the term broadly describes how a writer uses language to make a point. Some examples of rhetorical devices include repetition repeating words or phrases to call attention to their importance. Anaphora the repetition of a word or phrase as the beginning of sentences, clauses, or phrases, one after another, in a text. I believed in myself I believed in my team I believed in our mission. Anecdote a short retelling of a story to emphasize or reinforce a point the author is making. Alliteration a sound device in which the same beginning sound appears in many words near each other in a text. The dog was determined to defeat my desperate desire to remain detached. Allusion a reference to something separate from the text with the expectation that the audience recognizes it and understands its relevance. The conflict was reminiscent of David and Goliath. Emotional language used by authors to elicit desired emotions in their audience. Humor making jokes or other choices to feel more like the audience's friend. Negativity using harsh language to belittle the opposing side. Inclusion exclusion bringing the audience in by grouping them together or separating the others. Positive or negative connotations utilizing the connotation of a word to influence opinion. They are frugal with their resources versus they are stingy with their resources. Rhetorical questions questions that are not meant to be answered, but are used to encourage the audience to think, emphasize a point, or stir emotions. These can include textual choices rhetorical appeals. Use of evidence. Word choice. Performance choices stance. Gestures. Projection. Points of emphasis. Tone of voice.
Because rhetorical analysis is a challenging skill to master, teachers should model their own thinking about their chosen texts.
